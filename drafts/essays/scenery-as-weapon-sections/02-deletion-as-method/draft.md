# 2. Deletion as Method

Deletion is often narrated as housekeeping. The institutional vocabulary is familiar: duplication, migration, correction, rationalization. These terms suggest a neutral cleanup operation. But neutrality cannot be assumed when the burden of proof, document literacy, and access to officials are unevenly distributed.

At individual level, every deletion appears procedural. At population level, patterns become visible. A sequence of "routine" removals can produce neighborhood-scale disenfranchisement without any dramatic legal rupture. This is how administrative violence often works: dispersed, deniable, and cumulative.

To treat deletion seriously, analysis should move on three scales.

First, temporal scale. Compare cycles across years and election periods. Are there spikes tied to moments of heightened political contestation? Do removals accelerate before specific elections or after legal-administrative shifts?

Second, spatial scale. Cluster deletions by ward, polling part, and assembly segment. Do deletions concentrate in areas marked by specific settlement histories, linguistic profiles, or minority concentrations?

Third, relational scale. Compare deletion intensity against variables like overall population trend, migration indicators, and previous roll stability. If the archive's contraction is not matched by social movement on the ground, the discrepancy matters.

Methodological caution is essential.
- Name-based community inference must be probabilistic, not declarative.
- Missingness must be reported explicitly.
- Manual verification samples should be pre-registered where possible.
- Qualitative testimony should accompany pattern claims.

The point is not to accuse first and analyze later. The point is to make selective exclusion measurable enough to contest. In a majoritarian atmosphere, institutions often defend themselves through procedural opacity. A robust deletion analysis interrupts that opacity by making pattern legible, uncertainty visible, and accountability unavoidable.

Deletion, in this view, is not a clerical endpoint. It is an active political process through which a population can be thinned on paper while remaining materially present in place.
