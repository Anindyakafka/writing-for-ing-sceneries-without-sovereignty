# 4. Against Innocent Data

Data does not arrive from outside politics. It is born in forms, offices, legal categories, and infrastructures of extraction. Every dataset carries a production history: who collected, who classified, who dropped fields, who withheld publication, who re-labeled columns, and who decided what was not worth recording.

Calling data neutral is often a way of avoiding those histories.

A political method does not reject quantification. It rejects innocence. It asks how numbers were made and what they cannot say on their own. It treats absence as information and treats administrative language as an object of analysis rather than transparent truth.

In this project, three evidentiary streams should remain in dialogue.
- Historical record reconstruction from Bengali scanned rolls.
- Contemporary deletion pattern analysis in electoral lists.
- Censorboard decisions as cultural-governance traces.

None of these streams is complete by itself. Together they make a stronger argument about selective visibility.

Visual strategy matters here. A polished dashboard aesthetic can falsely suggest closure. D3 should instead be used to preserve friction.
- Layer events and policy shifts so causality is proposed, not asserted.
- Expose uncertainty bands and OCR confidence thresholds.
- Attach annotation as argument, not ornament.
- Make source links and methodological notes directly inspectable.

This approach keeps readers in contact with method. They can see where inference is strong, where it is tentative, and where more field evidence is needed.

The ethical center is simple: do not convert contested lives into clean graphics that erase the contest. Let the visual hold contradiction, incompleteness, and political stakes in the same frame.
