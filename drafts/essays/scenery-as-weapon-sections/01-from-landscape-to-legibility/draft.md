# 1. From Landscape to Legibility

States inherit land, but they do not rule it until they can read it.

Legibility is often mistaken for clarity. In practice, it is a selective technology. Administrative systems privilege what can be coded into columns and filtered by categories: residence, religion, age, eligibility, language labels, and geographies of jurisdiction. Whatever exceeds those labels is not merely ignored; it is pressured to translate itself into them.

This matters because the roll, register, and survey do not passively describe society. They reorganize social life by defining what counts as recognizable presence. A person may have lived in one locality for generations and still remain administratively precarious if document chains are unstable, script conventions shift, or local naming practices do not map cleanly onto standardized forms.

In West Bengal, electoral records can be read as archives of both continuity and interruption. The 2002 rolls, preserved largely as non-searchable Bengali scans, sit at a crucial historical moment: they capture naming patterns, settlement references, and local demographic traces before later rounds of digitization and standardization altered the evidentiary surface. A current deletion list from 2026 does something different. It records institutional withdrawal from prior recognition.

Read together, these two archives can support a rigorous question: not whether exclusion exists in the abstract, but where and how recognition contracts over time. If a locality's population remains materially dense while its electoral record becomes thinner, the mismatch itself demands investigation.

The technical barrier to this investigation is not incidental. OCR failure in Bengali-script PDFs is not only a machine problem; it shapes what can be publicly proven. If historical records stay visually present but computationally inaccessible, then asymmetry survives as anecdote instead of entering accountable analysis.

A collaborator's task here is methodological and political at once.
- Reconstruct machine-readable text from damaged scans.
- Preserve page-level references for audit trails.
- Build transparent confidence thresholds and manual checks.
- Keep uncertainty visible instead of smoothing it away.

Legibility, then, is not merely a property of state records. It can also be rebuilt from below as counter-legibility: a practice that restores names, localities, and continuities that administrative transitions tend to fragment.
